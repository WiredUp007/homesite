On 'To Kill A Mockingbird'

ps. The movie really kicked off my Gregory Peck obsession lol.

Kindness/Doing The Right Thing

It is easy to be kind to the one who you find pleasant, kind, agreeable and respectable- not so much when the person isn't any of that. And more so, is quite shitty.

TKAM argues for that kindness and respect to even those who you find disagreeable, those who you find the most unpleasant.

The scene with Mrs. Dubose really made me ponder over that again. And I think that's something we all would be done some good by learning. It's hard, all too hard, to be nice to the mean- but easy doesn't always mean right.

Bringing me to my last point for this essay..

Doing the right thing. The book, and its brilliant character Atticus Finch, are the argument for doing the right thing even when it ain't easy.

The novel doesn't shy away from portraying what people do for ease- to not speak up or participate in systematic issues, to go along for acceptance. Atticus is given hell for trying to defend an innocent man. And it is easy, to crumble under the system, and to be accepted by Whites, to not give it his all. But is not right. And Doing The Right Thing is practically Atticus Finch's middle name.

Either way. It ain't easy. Not for him, not for the family. Not for anyone who has an inkling of support for Tom and decides to voice it.

And yet, though all odds are stacked against you, though you know you are licked, you do the thing anyway.

Even if you goddamn know you are going to lose, you fight anyway.

And yet..

Its easy for a novel to go the dandy cynicism route and talk all bleak. It is easy to think the world is damned and everythin's evil and corrupt to core.

And yet the narrative has some final nudges for you..

There is change. Possibility of change. Flicker of change. Steps. Baby steps. There's goodness, inherent goodness. We just need time. It will just take a whole lot of time. But don't you give up. Don't you stop the good fight. Don't you shy away from doing the right and kind thing.

Because, at the end of the day, there's only one kind of folks. Folks.

The re-read makes me officially declare that Atticus Finch is not only my favorite character of all time, this novel is my favorite of all time. Good day.

history with this novel

I was maybe 14 when my elder cousin handed me her slightly worn out copy of this book. She said she didn't understand it. Maybe I could. I said alright and I read it. I understood the book and loved it. It touched my deeply, felt so special and I sort of went on to declare Atticus Finch to by my favorite fictional character of all time. Alright. Recency bias perhaps. Years passed by and he still held the place. But with those years, I sort of got a more blurry vision of what text was. I remembered the feel, the core incidents and how the characters were- but at age of near 19, it felt like a ripe old time for re-read and see if it held up the spot it had in my memory. So back from university on short break, I grabbed the copy from my bookshelf in home and spent the coming 24 hours with the single focus of finishing the novel.

so.. does it hold up? short answer? Yes! Long answer:

I was sent back to my time of first read. The book is still as good. Scout and Jem are still the charming children and Atticus is still that great, if not more. There are a lot of minute details I had forgotten from first read which were a delight to read again. From "the world is ending, Atticus! Please do something!" to the adult to adult conversations Scout often eavesdrops on.

And I have thoughts. A whole load of thoughts. The book is not just a hard look into how systematic racism perpetuated back then but on much, much more things. I have so many more topics I want to discuss. A single essay wouldn't be enough, but for the sake of practicality, let us divide those topics for today's discussion:
-only children will weep
-parent and children
-kindness/doing the right thing

Only Children Will Weep

I remembered that TKAM's brilliance lay in how you were made to see world through POV of two children, but I felt it again so deeply while reading it this time.

From thinking All Maycomb Folks Are Good Folks to Adults are (can be) cynics, ignorant, prejudiced, hateful; it is quite the journey. The Adult World is even stranger and the novel perfectly captures the childlike mind wandering through this. It perfectly captures the loss of innocence, the feeling of finding it all non sensical and unfair as you grow up.

There are moments which come to hit you right in gut; from Jem struggling and trying to grapple on the workings of world, to Dill getting sick to his stomach and crying at hearing the vicious way of questioning Tom, to Scout finding it too odd and wrong with how Miss Gates could hate Hitler but then turn right around and be prejudiced against some other class of people- it leaves you with discomfort. Much needed discomfort.

Parent And Child

This was, I think, the biggest theme I was reconnected to in my re-read. Throughout novel, alongside a whole load of other dilemmas, we see Atticus's best attempts at raising his children in the way he knows best. He is often criticised and often has his own doubts; but he knows he has to be the example. The book shows how important it is for the parent to be the right example. We see it happen slowly but soon, Jem is growing up to be like Atticus (No better man to be, am I right, folks?). And Scout has learned a few things herself.

Then, the trust. There is an unending trust, despite everything, that Atticus has their back, won't ever do them wrong and is the respite and haven from all ruin outside. Which is true, we see. Throughout the book, Atticus threatens with dry remarks or calls them out- but you never really see him do anything to them. He has never had the need to, he says, but he never would anyway.

(Its also mighty adorable how Scout is ready to throw hands and do anything to defend her father.)

The scene with mob having come for Tom and Atticus being in danger- Jem taking stand, not going. Scout sort of venturing with a childlike conversation with Cunningham. Alexenderia later calling them disgraces and Atticus going, he was right glad his disgraces were there. What a scene.
