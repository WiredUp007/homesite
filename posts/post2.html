Essay

On war, government, history and hope

As I sit here, in a room meant to house only one person and much colder than it needs to be, with other things at my hand which must be 'practically' more important than what I am writing— I replay some bits and pieces of my conversation from yesterday. I sip on water and I think; because that is the one thing I know how to do.

The person I was engaged with, mildly, was one of my decently good friends. She was lamenting on about how 'we', as in the 'general public', are prone to making humour and light of extremely serious and devastating situations these days. How we have become so 'desensitised' and nothing is really shocking ever, no matter the extent of that thing's cruelty.

She has been posting about all the current affairs; from Epstein files and their release, to the brewing tension of Israel-Iran-America conflict to the on-going genocide at Gaza.

I quite agree with her on most matters. I do not wish to slander her for being correct and empathetic. I do not wish to pinpoint her at all. Her example is one I pick because it is just the one most recent. What I wish to talk about is the hopelessness which awareness can bring.

It is a thing I have noticed among those who make themselves aware; awareness is good! It is brilliant and important and easily the first step to change. But awareness has a pitfall— it often leaves the one aware feeling as if there is little good in world and that we are doomed. Awareness leaves the aware one angry and questioning. Awareness leaves the aware one with less hope, with a sinking feeling of, 'well, this all sucks. World is heading down a dark path, we are all done for.'

Every person who has woken up to the state of things as they grew has gone or stayed in such a thought process, in which they quite give up on the good of it and can easily go down the merry road of awaiting doomsday. Which is, of course, a great concoction to be miserable. As an antidote or a prevention to this disease which comes with being aware, one might choose to be ignorant or choose to withdraw. Choosing ignorance as a path to happiness.

While one could do that, it does not help with change much either. That approach lets the status quo remain status quo. It accepts your 'minuteness' in this existence and gives into the fact that you cannot change much anyway, so why even try?

It is an easy way. A tempting one even. I understand those who turn to it. But I would not recommend it. In some other essay, I will or would have already argued on the need for goodness and purpose and putting some good into the world; that is one of my core beliefs. Purpose and doing some good. So. Let us say you carry the same belief and do not wish to be ignorant. Let us continue to talk about awareness. Now, all this awareness makes you feel; some of it is important. Here are the important components of your awareness, alongside its own self: Anger.

Anger is often painted in the colour red. "Discard your anger, humans! Be freer, be less angry, do not be controlled by the evil clutches of this emotion." Anger and ego of men have brought upon wars, crimes and vilest situations in history. Oh, its form is considered even one of the seven sins. And yet, I will argue for a point in a favour of anger. And keeping that anger. The anger your awareness brings— accept it. Accept the anger. Do away with the hopelessness. Then take that anger and channel it. Channel it to do what?

Doing good.

"It is the critical one who has dared to hope which has always brought change."

Do not let your awareness damp your hopes and your goodness and your wish to change the world. The world will change little by your efforts, mayhaps, but it will remain exactly the same for surety if you do nothing.

Anger is not just a component, I would argue, but it is the key itself which has driven change throughout centuries.

And do not ask yourself is it even worth it and is change even possible at this point.

Actually, you will. Everyone does.

So, before we talk about the exact use of anger, let's discuss if it is even worth being angry.

Every person who has been brought to awareness also has a tendency to forget that nothing is truly new. They think, this is the worst world has ever been and surely, it all is past salvation now. They think, this war, this part of history, this government- this time looks the darkest. This is the worst part of this nightmare and we are not waking up from it. They forget themselves and they undermine history.

If there is one thing which is sure about history is that it repeats itself and that humans never learn from it, but that they also forget about it. They forget that they have been at worse, that it is not the first time graph threatens to take a sharp spike low.

On the height of anti-Semitism and Nazism in Germany, journalist Sebastian Haffner wrote, ‘Everything takes place under a kind of anaesthesia. Objectively dreadful events produce a thin, puny emotional response. Murders are committed like schoolboy pranks. Humiliation and moral decay are accepted like minor incidents.’

It sounds much like our complains of desensitisation and that is because it is that— a light being thrown upon how people stop feeling the gravity of things that happen.

Concept of 'overexposure' and 'social media' overfeeding brain did not truly exist back then and yet, this pattern was the same. It is not the modernity of life then, which can be blamed completely for our current state and the way we perceive and act about things.

It is simply the nature of human, for when it is faced with so much dread and death, it goes into a trance where it stops taking everything 'to heart', for the lack of better wording.

Another example.

The way Artificial Intelligence threatens to displace man in numerous fields is again a distressing business— for myself, as well, I will admit. I am a writer and studying to be an engineer. Practically endangered on both ends— yet, I will remind you of its striking similarity to when industrial revolution happened and automation came to be. It was met with scrutiny, distrust, hostility and went on to displace a lot of manual labour. It feels new but it is not new. It is just history doing its course and human nature unfolding to it, reacting and frowning as always.

This brings me to the other point that history has never been an upward graph until now either always. Let us take human rights to be an example here.

Marginalised groups did not have rights. And all people and all thought must have been much regressive back then. Correct?

While true for a lot of population, history did not go from "this group did not have rights and was oppressed and hated by all and now it has rights but wait why are we going back"— more so, it always has been an up and down.

A 1920s newspaper column, "Inquiring Photographer", floats around. A 100 years back, the journalist stopped people on the street and asked them their thoughts. Simple questions such as, "Do you think it is degrading for a man to wash dishes or assist his wife in household duties?" or "Who should push the baby carriage, husband or wife?", alongside the obvious 'product-of-its-time' answers we would expect men and women of that time to give, a surprising amounts of answer were progressive even for today's standards. They said things which people debate even today upon and cannot seem to understand.

It is unfair to not credit history for its nuance.

All this to say, humankind right now, is neither at its best and nor at its worst. Humanity and history are just doing what they do— taking their natural course.

The wrong government taking over and the eventual sickness working class reaches with it. The murder and the war and the revolution.

..That isn't to argue that let the course be taken and shit go down. That is to say, that humans made it out multiple times out of hell before, and they will make it out back again.

Bleeding, fighting, dying; but they will.

Let us use some good fiction to illustrate these points.

Do not seek ignorance as a path to happiness and do not let awareness be a path to misery.

George Orwell's '1984' and Margaret Atwood's 'Handmaid's Tale' are considered staples of dystopian novels, and for good reason. They are fiction but border very close to portraying the real life. They are masterfully written cautionary tales.

People talk about them, but while talking, they forget one thing about them; their end.

1984, at the end, has an appendix which completely re contextualises the work. It is written by the point of view of a researcher long after the extremist regime of big brother fell. Similarly, the end of Atwood's novel features a conclave of researchers and historians conversing and discussing the government long after it was dismantled.

We do not learn how that happened but we do learn it happened; that unnatural state of surveillance, fear and control eventually came to an end. It was ugly and terrible, until it was not. It was the darkest before it was dawn.

This is not just fiction. This mirrors reality. When people say, 'these books are eerily similar to real life', they forget the hope these narratives have, in the most distilled manner.

Think about what all has happened in history. And think about fact that humanity made it out.

Think about the amount of kings which have been brought down and forced to abdicate. Think about the amount of dictators who looked as untouchable as gods but eventually fell. Think about the amount of system of harm and hate which were taken centuries to put into place and were brought down.

Think about the cruelty of being and the fight it eventually does against it.

So. Good enough reason to hope.

Change is always possible and will always happen. Well. Great. And I need my anger for it?

Yes. Yes, you do. You do not need to be angry all the time but you need to keep that anger, even if in a dormant and distilled manner, somewhere.

Let us pull back to the beginning and birth of revolution. Let us go back to France.

Between 1715 and 1789, french population grew by 8 million. Middle class increased in size. And despite overall increase in prosperity, the working class was dissatisfied. Most of the profits still lay with the top classes. Economic recession, bad harvests, and general unemployment led to anger and resentment. A feeling of being treated unfairly. A feeling of wanting change and dismantling the status quo. And that want for justice, that 'anger' is what sparked the long chain of events that would come to be known as revolution.

Or let us go a little ahead to the suffragette movement where the women of Britain, enraged and in wish for political equality, organised protests throughout years. Attacked, heckled, stormed and beat up politicians; that is anger. They stopped eating and went on hunger strike, only to be force-fed. Decades and finally, political equality was reached upon in the most surface level of manner.

Anger. They all had anger. And that was channelled for a resistance.

That is not to say attacking, heckling and beating up by going homes of powerful men is something you should be doing right at this moment; no, that is quite unrealistic as a sole entity. You do not need to channel your anger for violence.

Then, what can you do with this anger?

A good question.

( I personally wished to be the chief justice of my country and thought that after reaching that position, I had eradicate all the gender inequality and bring complete fairness to the world. Mighty ambitions. Reality struck. Quite unrealistic. Here I am. Doing something starkly different from those ideas. And yet, I think I am doing something. Can do something. Writing this piece firstly is my vessel to use the anger in me. )

The very very first thing you can do is practice radical kindness. Start being nice to people. Start being kind. Stop looking down on people for being different from you. Stop thinking yourself as morally superior for having read a few more news articles or writing this piece (this is, indeed, a dig at myself)— Even at the most hateful man you know, look at him as if he is a human.

I can (and will, probably) write another essay on the necessity to not discard humanity away of even those who we consider the spawn of devil themselves but let us move on.

Understand them, where they come from— do not agree. Do not appreciate either. It is only understanding you need to equip you with better kindness and better awareness.

Second thing you can do is start talking about things. Discuss, converse and debate. Organise discussions, make collectives, produce a safe space; the kindness you were showing to a friend now gets extended to mayhaps 5 more people. Still a lot.

Artists need to keep creating art more than ever. Creation is respite. Creation is revolution.

Thirdly, if you earn and wish to truly do something, try activism. NGOs, collectives and organisations which work on a mass-level. Donate and volunteer. Heck, you do not need to be earning to volunteer (this is another note to myself moment).

History isn't at its worst. It is taking a very natural course. And you must take your own natural course in it.

"That the powerful play goes on and you may contribute a verse."

Your anger is not to be stripped away. Your awareness need not be a pathway to misery.

Make it your sword. Your instrument. Your pen. Your brush.

Fight. Sing. Write. Paint.

Breathe.

And go on now, you may contribute your verse.
