On 'To Kill A Mockingbird'

ps. The movie really kicked off my Gregory Peck obsession lol.

I watched Roman Holiday and Spellbound under 2 days just because.. well. I know. I can seem far gone. Story for another day.

history with this novel

I was maybe 14 when my elder cousin handed me her slightly worn out copy of this book. She said she didn't understand it. Maybe I could. I said alright and I read it. I understood the book and loved it. It touched my deeply, felt so special and I sort of went on to declare Atticus Finch to by my favorite fictional character of all time. Alright. Recency bias perhaps. Years passed by and he still held the place. But with those years, I sort of got a more blurry vision of what text was. I remembered the feel, the core incidents and how the characters were- but at age of near 19, it felt like a ripe old time for re-read and see if it held up the spot it had in my memory. So back from university on short break, I grabbed the copy from my bookshelf in home and spent the coming 24 hours with the single focus of finishing the novel.

so.. does it hold up? short answer? Yes! Long answer:

I was sent back to my time of first read. The book is still as good. Scout and Jem are still the charming children and Atticus is still that great, if not more. There are a lot of minute details I had forgotten from first read which were a delight to read again. From "the world is ending, Atticus! Please do something!" to the adult to adult conversations Scout often eavesdrops on.

And I have thoughts. A whole load of thoughts. The book is not just a hard look into how systematic racism perpetuated back then but on much, much more things. I have so many more topics I want to discuss. A single essay wouldn't be enough, but for the sake of practicality, let us divide those topics for today's discussion:
-only children will weep
-parent and children
-kindness/doing the right thing
